China, then Syracuse

I studied psychology at Fudan University in Shanghai. If you do not know the Chinese schools: QS ranks Fudan around 30th in the world, the same neighborhood as the Ivy League. I learned why people do what they do before I learned what a funnel was, and it is still the most useful thing I studied. Then a communications degree at Syracuse, where I learned to say the thing plainly.

One more thing about that chapter: it is not easy for a Chinese graduate who is not an engineer to stay in the US. Of the Chinese classmates I knew, none managed to stay. The visa math does not favor a psychology-and-communications kid. I stayed anyway, on work that proved itself.

The incubator years

My first real chapter was CP Baker, an incubator in Boston that owned very different companies. I started in design, taught myself to code, and shipped the companies' websites in HTML, PHP and JavaScript. Then I ran digital marketing across the portfolio: a shampoo and a nutrition bar sold into Whole Foods and Costco, a wholesale business moving bulk daily work goods, an IP company that owned a film library, including one of Bruce Lee's films, and a finance-education firm. Every company was a new industry, so reading a new business fast stopped being a skill and became the job.

That range is why a six-brand unit does not overwhelm me today. I have been the new guy in a strange industry many times, and I know how to find the parts that matter.

Boston Institute of Finance: $0 to $5M, then an acquisition

One portfolio company became the focus: Boston Institute of Finance, education for wealth-management firms, registered investment advisors and banks. Regulated finance, where a compliance mistake does not cost you a deal, it costs you the license. When we took the company over it had essentially no business.

I grew into running it. By the end I was the director of revenue operations and essentially the chief operating officer: everyone except the CEO ran on my plan, and everyone was ten people, counting the admin and the accountant. With that headcount I owned which education products we built, the demand generation and retention systems, the reporting, and the budget, so every experiment had a cost and an owner attached.

We took revenue from nothing to $5M, and Colibri Group acquired the company. I led the integration: carrying the team, the products and the systems into the buyer without dropping the revenue. Ten people to $5M in a regulated market is the proudest math of my career. The plan was mine, and so was the machine under it.

Colibri: six brands, one brain

Today I am Director of Business Operations for a $100M unit at Colibri Group, professional education in regulated industries, where every one of our six brands answers to a different licensing rulebook. The unit sits inside an ecosystem of about 300 people. My direct team is about 25, and roughly 150 people across marketing, sales and engineering run on the roadmaps and plans my team sets. The job is planning, timelines, keeping six brands collaborating, and pivoting fast when the data says pivot.

Six brands means six industries, six workflows, six vocabularies. No amount of status meetings keeps that in one head, so I built the unit a brain instead. Here is the thinking: an AI model is a good reasoner and a terrible place to store the truth. It forgets, and you cannot inspect what it believes. So the brain is not the model. The brain is a plain, versioned knowledge base the model reasons against: how each brand works, what its terms mean, who owns what, which decisions were made and why. In practice it is plain files under version control, readable by me and by the agents, no vendor in the middle. The agents read from it while I work, and whatever a conversation teaches me gets written back in the same day, so the system knows more every week than it did the week before. Documents start accurate instead of starting blank.

The same rule runs the human side. If every team looks at its own data, you never get anywhere, so a large part of my job is making six brands agree on one set of numbers. The brain is how I navigate the context. The shared numbers are how the teams navigate each other.

A market map that refreshes itself

One build from inside that unit, in full. For our insurance-education brand I needed to rank the market: which companies employ the most licensed insurance agents. My first version ranked them by a dropout story, how many agents wash out and when. The data would not carry that thesis, so I killed it and ranked by plain headcount instead.

The build is a table in Clay, a go-to-market data tool, that reads the Texas insurance regulator's public data feed directly, adds the counts up per company, and re-pulls the feed monthly. Nothing is pasted in, so it does not go stale, and nobody has to maintain it. The map surfaced 46 insurance carriers that all sell through the same distributor, and that finding set the segmentation and the outreach plan for the brand.

How I lead

Clear is kind. I communicate directly, never down at someone, and data is my weapon. When we disagree, we go find the data. When the data does not exist, we run a test, and the test settles it. If I am wrong, I am wrong. I am not shy about being proved wrong, and the team knows it, which is exactly why the wins carry weight.

Every fact on this page is mine, and I checked each one. What the loop tested is whether you would understand them.
