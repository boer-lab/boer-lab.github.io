BOSTON INSTITUTE OF FINANCE · 2018–2024

$0 to $5M, then an acquisition

Boston Institute of Finance: education for wealth-management firms, registered investment advisors and banks. This is regulated finance: one compliance mistake can cost the company its license to operate. The company had essentially no business when we took it over.

By the end I ran it as director of revenue operations and de facto COO. Everyone except the CEO ran on my plan, and the whole company was ten people, counting everyone. Product, demand generation, retention, reporting, budget: one owner.

Ten people to $5M in a regulated market is the proudest math of my career. The plan was mine, and so was the machine under it.

COLIBRI GROUP · 2024–TODAY

Six brands, one memory

Today I am Director of Business Operations for a $100M unit at Colibri Group, professional education in regulated industries. My direct team is about 25. Roughly 150 people across marketing, sales and engineering run on the plans my team sets.

The six brands all live in financial services, and they are still drastically different businesses: wealth management, insurance, banking, each with its own model, workflow and vocabulary. Six businesses do not fit in anyone's head, and meetings do not scale. So I built the unit a shared memory. AI models are good at thinking and bad at remembering: ask twice, get two answers, and you cannot check what one believes. So the memory lives outside the model, as a plain, versioned knowledge base: how each brand works, what its terms mean, who owns what, which decisions were made and why. The AI reads it before answering and writes back what we learned the same day, so it knows more every week than it did the week before. And because shared numbers are the same discipline, a large part of my job is making six brands agree on one set of them.

WORK SAMPLE · BUILT IN CLAY · SUMMER 2026

A go-to-market engine that runs itself

Every sales team needs the same engine underneath: who is out there, which of them are worth the effort, and a target list that stays current without anyone babysitting it. This is that engine, built end to end in Clay, a tool for go-to-market data systems, during a four-week GTM engineering program this summer. The market is one I know well: insurance education, where the question is which agencies employ the most licensed agents.

The engine reads the Texas insurance regulator's public data feed, does the counting on the government's own servers, filters out empty shells, expired licenses and one-person shops, scores every agency 0 to 100 on fit, and re-pulls the feed monthly. Nothing is pasted in. Nobody maintains it. I tested a dropout-rate thesis first; the data said no, so the engine ranks on headcount. The number decides, even when what it kills is my own idea.

And I am tool agnostic. Clay was the right tool for this one. Give me a different industry and a different stack, and the same thinking builds the same engine. The tool is never the point. The thinking is.

HOW I LEAD · THREE RULES

Three rules

Data-driven

When we disagree, we go find the data. When there is no data, we run a test, and the test settles it. People stop guessing when they trust the number in front of them.

Clear is kind

I say the thing directly, never down at someone. Feedback comes early and often, and it goes both ways: if I am wrong, I am wrong, and the team knows it.

Put people where they win

Find each person's strengths and weaknesses, learn what fuels them, and give them direction that way. Someone doing the work their strength is built for is good for everyone.
